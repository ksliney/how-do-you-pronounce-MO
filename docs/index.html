Debate: Is it Missour-ee or Missour-ah?

September 1, 2020


By Kate Sliney




Before the topic of wearing a mask became a politically-charged activity, another seemingly benign issue also sparked political debate—the pronunciation of a centrally-located US state. If you guessed, “Nevada?”— guess again — Nevadans overwhelmingly agree it's “Nev-ada,” (though the President Trump, does not).


Rather, it’s in the “Show-me state,” among the practically minded inhabitants to whom the state legendarily owes its nickname, where the debate over “Missouree” versus “Missourah” rages on as a mostly good-natured (but still politcally charged) topic.


It may surprise some that this debate goes back over century, and as such, many articles have been written on differences in Missourians’ preferred pronunciation, including politicans’ routine struggle to choose a pronunciation that seems authentic and signals the right message, yet doesn’t alienate voters who prefer the other pronunciation, during an election year.


What is unclear, is exactly how linguistic preferences break down among different groups living in Missouri today (e.g. among urban versus rural residents, democrats versus republicans, and eastern versus western Missouri residents) and whether the choice to use “missour-ee” versus “missour-ah” significantly correlates with any of these groups, especially given declines in regional accents nationwide. And even if the choice to use “Missour-ee” versus “Missour-ah” does is significantly divided between certain groups—does it matter to them which pronunciation others choose to use?
